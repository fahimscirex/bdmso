Twelve primary school students spent a full day doing real science. Not reading about it, not watching videos - actually doing it. The BdMSO Lab Day brought together a small group and gave them access to equipment and experiments that most primary students in Bangladesh never get to experience.

## The Experiments

**Skeletal Mapping**

Students were given printed diagrams and physical models to map the major bones of the human skeleton. The exercise connected biology theory to something tangible, and the conversations about how different bones protect different organs were some of the most engaged moments of the day.

**Light and Prisms**

Using prisms and a focused light source, students split white light into its component colours and observed how different materials bend light differently. Several students immediately connected this to rainbows - which led to a ten-minute discussion about why the sky is blue.

**Microscopes**

Students prepared slides and looked at onion skin cells, pond water, and their own hair under microscopes. Seeing cells for the first time - actual cells, not diagrams - was a turning point for many. One student kept asking to look at different things around the room under the lens.

**Elephant Toothpaste**

The day's showstopper: a chemical reaction between hydrogen peroxide and yeast that produces a rapid, dramatic foam. Students predicted what would happen before the reaction, observed it happening, then discussed why - decomposition, catalysts, and exothermic reactions.

## Why It Matters

The IMSO Science paper includes an experimental component. Students who have never held lab equipment are at a significant disadvantage when faced with a timed experiment. Lab Day is BdMSO's way of closing that gap - giving students exposure to the process of science, not just the content.
